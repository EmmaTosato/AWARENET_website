Interdisciplinary research that keeps humans in the loop

Mission

Understanding how the brain gives rise to consciousness is one of the central challenges in neuroscience.
AWARENET aims to build an integrated atlas of consciousness, combining fine-grained intracranial data with large-scale brain organization to explain how awareness emerges and how it can be disrupted by brain lesions.
Using stereo-EEG recordings and stimulations in humans, AWARENET links the level of consciousness (how awake or responsive the brain is) and the content of consciousness (what we perceive) within a common spatial framework.
This unified Awareness Atlas serves both fundamental science and clinical practice, supporting diagnosis and rehabilitation in conditions such as stroke, epilepsy, coma, and vegetative state.

Main challenges

Our dataset relies on intracranial recordings (sEEG) from drug-resistant epilepsy patients, providing high spatial and temporal resolution but no direct fMRI data, raising a key question: How can we link functional connectivity with consciousness when no direct fMRI is available?

To overcome this limitation, we:

  • Embed individual lesions into normative connectomes, enabling indirect mapping of disconnection.
  • Combine structural (dMRI) and functional (fMRI) gradients from healthy populations.
  • Develop predictive models linking lesion impact to consciousness loss and recovery.

What we aim to do

AWARENET will:

  • Map brain regions supporting level and content of consciousness through intracranial perturbations and recordings (sEEG).
  • Integrate these maps with large-scale cortical gradients derived from normative fMRI/dMRI datasets (HCP 7T).
  • Test causality by studying patients undergoing controlled thermocoagulation lesions, comparing behavioral decline with disconnection-overlap in the atlas.
  • Develop tools that predict how specific lesions alter awareness, bridging basic and clinical neuroscience.
